Abstract—Retrieval Augmented Generation significantly improves factual answer generation in Large Language Models (LLMs), yet these systems remain susceptible to hallucination and generally lack any mechanism to verify if the retrieved evidence supports generated outputs. Existing literature focuses on retrieval optimization and coarse-grained evaluation, leaving a critical gap in fine-grained output evaluation. Our approach is a two phase framework designed for claim-level factual verification of generated output. Phase I implements an inverted question matching pipeline [3] to accurately generate answers based on the relevant text chunks. Phase II introduces a novel trust verification layer that decomposes these generated answers into atomic claims. It then verifies each claim individually against retrieved chunks and labels them as SUPPORTED, CONTRADICTED, or NOT_FOUND. To implement this, we introduce a novel system, CLARA (Claim-Level Atomic Reasoning and Abstention). This system calculates a similarity weighted confidence score, and a trust gated router dynamically decides whether to output the verified answer or safely abstain based on a set threshold. We evaluated our model on BERTScore and RAGAS evaluation metrics and achieved an F1 of 0.8675 on in-scope queries and a 94.12% abstention precision on out-of-scope queries, which showcases that the model performs well on both in-scope and out-of-scope queries. Ultimately, this framework successfully provides a RAG system that provides fine-grained answer verification and a trustworthy, self-regulating system that is capable of safe abstention in case of insufficient evidence.
I.  INTRODUCTION
Large Language Models (LLMs) have changed the way we interact with information by generating human-like answers to questions. They are widely used in applications like chatbots, education tools, and search systems. However, when it comes to answering domain specific or factual questions, these models are not always reliable. They can sometimes generate incorrect or made-up information (hallucinations), even when the answer sounds confident and correct. This creates a major problem, especially when users depend on these systems for accurate information.
To solve this issue, two common approaches are used: fine tuning and Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG). Fine tuning improves the model by training it on specific data, but it requires a lot of resources and time. It can also cause the model to forget previously learned information. On the other hand, RAG uses external data by retrieving relevant documents and then generating answers based on them. This makes it more flexible and practical for real world use.
Although RAG improves answer quality, it still has some important limitations. The biggest problem is that it does not check whether the generated answer is supported by the retrieved data. The model may still include wrong or unrelated information. It also does not handle situations where the answer is not available in the data, and it continues to generate responses with high confidence. This reduces trust in the system.
Recent methods like QuIM-RAG [3] and other verification based approaches [14] try to improve retrieval and answer quality. QuIM-RAG focuses on better matching between questions and stored data, which improves retrieval accuracy. However, even these methods do not fully solve the problem of trust. They do not verify each part of the generated answer, do not detect contradictions properly, and do not decide when to avoid answering.
In this work, we propose a QuIM-RAG Extension, CLARA, which adds a trust verification layer to the existing pipeline. After generating an answer, the system breaks it into small claims, checks each claim with the retrieved data, and assigns labels like supported or not found. Then, a confidence score is calculated to decide whether the answer should be shown or the system should safely avoid answering. This approach helps in reducing wrong answers and improves the overall reliability of the system. The main motivation of this work is to make RAG systems more trustworthy by not only generating answers but also verifying them before showing them to users.

While LLMs such as GPT and LLaMA exhibit strong generative performance, they remain prone to hallucination and factual errors. RAG mitigates this by retrieving supportive evidence, but retrieval fidelity, verification granularity, and system trustworthiness still pose challenges. 2.1 Retrieval Optimization and Pipeline Enhancements
Efficient retrieval forms the backbone of an effective RAG system. Multi stage retrieval pipelines, such as the five stage Multi Stage RAG [1], achieve strong contextual relevance through semantic chunking and abstract first retrieval. Similarly, EvaRAG [9] empirically explores indexing (HNSW, IVF) and similarity metrics, refining end-to-end retrieval reliability.
The quality of chunking and embeddings significantly affects outcomes. For example: Semantic chunking [13] mitigates weak boundary segmentation, embedding quality analyses [11] link vector performance to hallucination rates, and financial RAG [10] enhances retrieval through multi query fusion and rank re-weighting.
Domain specific retrieval optimization (e.g., DR-RAG [4]) integrates knowledge graphs and rules for interpretability, while Causal RAG [12] counteracts irrelevant evidence using causal reasoning.
2.2 Query Understanding and Advanced Retrieval Mechanisms
To strengthen alignment between user intent and document retrieval, methods like QuIM-RAG [3] introduce inverted question matching, aligning user queries with question representations rather than raw text. Extending this, ROQuA [2] applies clustering and iterative ranking to improve retrieval in opinion based QA.
Moreover, Speculative RAG [8] incorporates a drafter verifier loop, validating partial responses mid generation, while CATS-RAG [19] synthesizes hard negative QA triplets to increase retriever resilience. Despite progress, these models still rely heavily on the LLM’s internal verifier, leaving gaps in factual trust.
2.3 Hallucination Reduction and Verification Mechanisms
Reducing hallucination remains a central concern. Studies such as RAG using Wikipedia grounding [5] show that retrieval based context improves answer precision. RAC [17] extends this by performing post generation correction at the claim-level, verifying atomic facts and revising errors.
Further developments include:
i. VERIFAID [14]   zero-shot fact-checking via dynamically enriched datasets;
ii. Legal RAGs [6] [7] incorporating feedback and evaluation agents.
iii. Rule based reasoning [4] and Speculative RAG [8] validating intermediate outputs.
Nonetheless, verification in most frameworks remains coarse-grained, typically validating an entire answer, not its individual assertions.
2.4 Research Gap and Motivation
From previous literature, it is evident that while many systems enhance either retrieval depth or verification robustness, few achieve both, particularly not at the claim-level. This is demonstrated in Table 1. Most of the systems predominantly rely on coarse level verification rather than fine-grained claim analysis.

In response, this research introduces the CLARA, a Generate Verify Route pipeline designed to achieve fine-grained trust assessment. Its core principles include response decomposition into atomic claims, per-claim verification against retrieved evidence and confidence based routing and abstention mechanisms.
This design enables RAG systems to reason with trust, delivering higher transparency, reduced hallucination, and improved safety in open domain and domain specific deployments.

The proposed method is organised into two sequential phases. Phase I realises the core QuIM RAG pipeline, which handles corpus preparation, hypothetical question guided vector indexing, and constrained answer generation. Phase II introduces the trust verification pipeline, which subjects every preliminary answer to atomic claim decomposition, per-claim semantic verification, mathematically grounded confidence scoring, and confidence based output routing. Together, the two phases address the dual failure modes that affect conventional RAG systems: the semantic asymmetry between interrogative user queries and declarative document passages, and the susceptibility of large language models (LLMs) to generating content that is inconsistent with the retrieved context. A.  Phase I: Inverted Question Matching RAG (QuIM-RAG)
1) Web Crawling and Data Ingestion
The pipeline is seeded with a set of target URLs drawn from the institutional corpus of interest in the present instantiation, the NDSU Career and Advising Center portal (career-advising.ndsu.edu) and the NDSU Academic Catalogue (catalog.ndsu.edu). A breadth first crawler traverses all internally linked pages within the permitted domain set, observing a 1.0 s inter request delay to remain compliant with standard web crawling conventions. Each retrieved HTML response is processed by a content isolation routine that removes navigational chrome, header, footer, sidebar, script, and style elements via BeautifulSoup's DOM traversal interface, reducing each page to its semantic body text. Pages whose extracted text falls below a 250 character threshold are discarded as substantively empty, and pages whose HTML title element contains the string '404' are filtered to eliminate error pages that passed the HTTP status check. Retained pages are serialised to a line delimited JSON store, with each record carrying the originating URL and character count as provenance metadata.
2) Text Chunking
The extracted text of each crawled page is divided into fixed length, overlapping segments by a TikToken based chunker operating on the cl100k base subword vocabulary (LLaMA3-8b). Each segment spans at most 1,000 tokens. A 200 character character level overlap is maintained between consecutive segments to prevent hard truncation at phrase or sentence boundaries, preserving semantic continuity across adjacent chunks. Every resulting chunk is assigned to a unique sequential identifier (chunk_id) and retains its originating source URL as metadata, establishing an unbroken provenance chain from raw crawled page to retrievable passage.
3) Hypothetical Question Generation
   The central architectural innovation of the QuIM-RAG paradigm is that retrieval is performed in the question semantic space rather than the document semantic space. To realize this, for each text chunk c, an instruction following model is prompted to enumerate all distinct natural language questions that the chunk can adequately answer. This phase uses LLaMA3-8b, operated at a temperature of 0.3 to balance generative diversity with factual fidelity. The prompt instructs the model to cover every key informational unit within the chunk, to avoid redundant or overlapping formulations, and to return exactly one question per output line with no numbering or annotation, facilitating deterministic line by line parsing.
The resulting question set Q_c = {q_1, q_2, …, q_n} is stored together with the chunk's full text and source URL in a corpus record, forming the indexed corpus on which Phase II depends. This step directly eliminates the query passage distributional gap: at retrieval time, the user's question is compared not to declarative passage text, but to other questions, ensuring that the embedding model operates within a homogeneous interrogative semantic space rather than bridging across register boundaries.
4) Dual Collection Vector Indexing
The generated questions and their corresponding raw chunk texts are embedded into dense vector representations using the BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5 bi-encoder, which held the top position on the Massive Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB) English retrieval track at the time of system construction. All embeddings are L2 normalized before storage, so that cosine similarity and dot product become algebraically equivalent at query time. The dense vectors are persisted in a ChromaDB instance configured with an HNSW approximate nearest neighbor index under a cosine distance metric.
Critically, two logically distinct collections are maintained within the same ChromaDB store. The question collection stores one embedding per generated question, each tagged with the chunk_id of the passage from which it was derived. The chunk collection stores one embedding per raw chunk text, keyed by the same chunk_id. The chunk_id, therefore, functions as a foreign key linking the two collections. This dual collection design cleanly separates the retrieval search space (question embeddings) from the generation context store (raw chunk text), while keeping both co located within a single persistent vector database.
5) Question to Question Retrieval
At inference time, a user query Q is encoded using the same BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5 model with the BGE retrieval instruction prefix prepended: "Represent this sentence for searching relevant passages." The resulting query embedding is matched against the question collection using cosine similarity, and the top k = 3 most similar indexed questions are retrieved. The chunk_id values attached to these matched questions are then extracted from their ChromaDB metadata and deduplicated since multiple generated questions may originate from the same source chunk before being used to fetch the corresponding raw chunk texts from the chunk collection. The retrieved chunk texts and their associated source URLs constitute the grounding context C = {c_1, c_2, …, c_k} supplied to the generation stage. This two hop lookup query embedding → question match → chunk retrieval is the operational realization of the inverted index.
6) Constrained Answer Generation
The retrieved context C and the original query Q are assembled into a structured generation prompt supplied to a locally hosted instance of Meta-LLaMA3-8B, served via the Ollama inference runtime. The prompt enforces three hard constraints: (i) the model must base its answer exclusively on the provided context passages and must not draw on parametric knowledge that cannot be traced to the supplied text; (ii) if the retrieved context is insufficient to answer the query, the model must say so explicitly rather than confabulate; and (iii) source URLs must be cited at the end of the response. The generation temperature is set to 0.1 to favor factual consistency over creative elaboration strongly. The output of this stage is a preliminary answer A′ that is semantically plausible and context grounded but has not yet undergone formal claim-level verification.
B.  Phase II: CLARA Post Generation Verification
Despite the retrieval grounded prompt, LLMs can still generate statements that are not strictly derivable from the supplied context, particularly when context passages contain partial information that invites implicit completion. Phase II addresses this through a structured three stage post processing pipeline applied to every preliminary answer A′ before it is returned to the user.
1) Atomic Claim Decomposition
Holistic verification of a multi sentence natural language answer is both computationally intractable and semantically ill defined. A far more tractable approach decomposes the answer into a set of atomic claims {c_1, c_2, …, c_N}, each containing exactly one independently verifiable factual assertion. The decomposition module prompts LLaMA3-8B-Instruct at temperature 0.0 (fully deterministic) to rewrite A′ as a numbered list of atomic claims, subject to the constraints that each claim encapsulates precisely one checkable fact, that no new information is introduced beyond what appears in the original answer, and that the total claim count is bounded at N_max = 12 to limit verification overhead. The model output is parsed by extracting numbered or bulleted lines and stripping formatting characters; duplicate claims are removed by case insensitive comparison. If the model output is unparseable, for instance, due to a partial generation under memory pressure, a deterministic sentence boundary splitter serves as a fallback, ensuring that the pipeline degrades gracefully.
2) Per Claim Semantic Verification
Each atomic claim c_i is independently evaluated against the context chunks E = {e_1, …, e_k} retrieved during Phase I. Verification proceeds in two stages.
In the first stage, a semantic similarity ranking is computed. The claim c_i and each context chunk e_j are encoded using the shared BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5 embedder, and pairwise cosine similarities are computed. The top n_e = 3 highest scoring evidence chunks are selected as the evidentiary grounding for c_i, and their texts are concatenated into a structured evidence block. This targeted, claim-level retrieval ensures that each claim is evaluated against the most relevant available evidence, rather than against the full retrieved context, which may contain passages informative for other claims but ambiguous or uninformative for the claim under evaluation.
   In the second stage, the claim and its associated evidence block are presented to an LLM judge again LLaMA3-8B at temperature 0.0, with the instruction to assign exactly one categorical verdict from the label set:
• SUPPORTED - the claim is directly and explicitly substantiated by the evidence.
• CONTRADICTED - the claim is directly and explicitly refuted by the evidence.
• NOT FOUND - the evidence neither substantiates nor refutes the claim; the assertion may be true, but cannot be confirmed from the available retrieved context.
The judge is additionally required to supply a concise one sentence rationale for its verdict, which is stored in the verification record for downstream transparency and, in the case of abstention, is surfaced directly to the end user as part of the audit trail.
3) Trust Scoring
To quantify the overall reliability of the preliminary answer A′, the system computes two complementary confidence metrics over the N verified claims.
Base Confidence Score (Sconf).  The raw confidence score is defined as the fraction of claims that receive the SUPPORTED verdict:
S_conf  =  |{ cᵢ ∈ C  :  L(cᵢ) = SUPPORTED }|  /  N    (1)
S_conf is a coarse, binary vote measure. It treats all supported claims as equally trustworthy regardless of the strength of their evidentiary grounding, and is therefore useful as a lower bound trust signal.
Weighted Confidence Score (Sweighted).  To penalize claims that are labelled SUPPORTED based on only tangentially related evidence, a similarity weighted metric is introduced. The contribution of each supported claim to the numerator is scaled by its best evidence cosine similarity, while unsupported claims contribute zero to the numerator, but their similarity scores still appear in the denominator, penalizing responses that rely on weakly related context:
S_weighted  =  Σ_{L(cᵢ)=SUPPORTED} max(0, sim(cᵢ, E))  /  Σ_{cᵢ∈C} max(0, sim(cᵢ, E))    (2)
Negative cosine similarities—arising when a claim and its best matching evidence chunk are semantically antonymic or orthogonal—are floored to zero to prevent sign reversal in the numerator. S_weighted is employed as the primary routing signal.
4)  Confidence Gate and Output Routing
The final component of the pipeline is a confidence gate governed by a predefined threshold τ, empirically set to 0.75 in the present work. Let A(Q) denote the final system output for query Q. The routing decision is:
A(Q) = VERIFIED_ANSWER ∪ {per claim citations} if S_weighted ≥ τ
                ABSTENTION ∪ {failed claim audit trail} if  S_weighted < τ    (3)
When the VERIFIED path is activated, the user receives the preliminary answer A′ together with a per claim citation record. For every claim c_i, the record includes the source URL, the chunk identifier, the cosine similarity to its best matching evidence, and the verification label, providing granular, independently auditable provenance for each factual assertion in the response.
When the ABSTENTION path is activated, the system explicitly declines to answer and returns a structured audit trail comprising, for each failed claim: the claim text, its assigned label (CONTRADICTED or NOT_FOUND), the LLM judge's one sentence rationale, and the source URL of the best matching, but ultimately insufficient evidence chunk. This output is deliberately more informative than a simple refusal: it enables the user to understand precisely which sub claims could not be verified and why, facilitating targeted follow up or manual review.
The choice of τ = 0.75 represents a deliberate precision recall tradeoff. A higher threshold reduces the probability of serving partially unverified responses (lower false acceptance rate) at the cost of a higher false refusal rate on queries whose answers are genuinely supportable but happen to involve claims with moderate similarity scores. Systematic threshold calibration on a held out validation set is recommended for domain specific deployments.
IV.  RESULTS
The evaluation of the framework was conducted using a custom dataset of 50 queries, consisting of 30 in-scope questions (domain specific to the NDSU corpus) and 20 out-of-scope questions. The framework’s performance was assessed categorically across core pipeline evaluation, abstention and out-of-scope query handling, and factual verification (Trust Pipeline).
A. Core Pipeline Evaluation
The system, comprising atomic claim decomposition, per claim semantic verification, and confidence-gated routing, was evaluated separately using a scoped evaluation framework. The 50 ground truth questions were manually partitioned into 30 in-scope and 20 out-of-scope queries, where out-of-scope questions referred to institutional details absent from the corpus. 
The in-scope questions were evaluated using BERTScore [21] and RAGAS [22] because these methods are more appropriate and tailored for our RAG-based question answering context. These evaluation results are listed in Table 3. BERTScore F1 yielded a score of 0.8675, with a Recall of 0.9273, demonstrating strong semantic fidelity to the source texts. The system achieved a Faithfulness score of 0.8254, with Context Recall of 0.9833, indicating that the retrieval pipeline successfully isolated the necessary context for the vast majority of in-scope queries.

D. Qualitative Analysis of Answer Quality
Beyond quantitative metrics, a qualitative review of the generated responses highlights the system’s strong contextual reasoning and efficient handling of complex queries. For in-scope domain questions, the system consistently produced answers that were not only factually accurate but also structured logically, synthesizing information from multiple retrieved chunks without losing semantic coherence. The model demonstrated a high adherence to the retrieved context, successfully avoiding the introduction of external, unverified knowledge (hallucinations) in the majority of evaluated cases. 
For the out-of-scope questions, the system correctly identified the absence of grounding evidence and abstained via the confidence router in most of the cases. However, the qualitative analysis also provided insight into the 0.2 False Answer Rate observed during testing. In these minority cases where the system struggled to decline unsupported queries, the errors typically occurred when out-of-scope prompts shared dense lexical overlap with in-scope concepts. In such instances, the model attempted to generate an answer from related chunks rather than triggering an abstention response. Despite these occurrences, even the “false” answers generally maintained a conservative tone, indicating that the system’s safety standards partially mitigated the severity of the hallucinations. However, further enhancement of the confidence router is required for strict refusal on borderline queries, which points to a calibration opportunity in future work.
V.  CONCLUSION
This paper presented an implementation and extension of the QuIM-RAG framework, CLARA, applied to a custom institutional knowledge base derived from North Dakota State University’s Career and Advising Centre website. The baseline implementation faithfully reproduced the inverted question matching retrieval mechanism described by Saha et al., employing BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5 embeddings, ChromaDB as the vector store, and Meta LLaMA3-8B as the generation model. 
The principal novel contribution of our work is the trust verification extension comprising four new pipeline stages: atomic claim decomposition, per claim verification against retrieved evidence, trust scoring based on supported claims, and a confidence-gated output router. This extension addresses a fundamental limitation of standard RAG systems, i.e. not being able to distinguish evidentially supported answers from plausible but ungrounded ones. The CLARA pipeline demonstrated strong performance on the in-scope evaluation track (BERTScore F1 = 0.8675, RAGAS Answer Relevancy = 0.8254) and achieved an abstention recall of 0.8 on out-of-scope questions. These results verify that the verification mechanism is highly effective at suppressing hallucinated responses when the retrieved context is insufficient. 
Ultimately, this approach successfully transitions standard RAG methodologies from passive context aggregators to self-regulating, evidence based systems. Future work may explore the refinement of false answer boundaries, aiming to reduce the False Answer Rate while maintaining the high generation yields. We will also increase the corpus size and introduce a more diverse dataset. This approach is a step in advancing the integration of trustworthy generative models in critical institutional applications.
